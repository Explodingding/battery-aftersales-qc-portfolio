Eight Gates Before Release: Quality Control for Repaired Lithium-Ion Battery Packs

Lessons from battery manufacturing and Brainport production — applied to aftersales · Łukasz Klimowski

The problem nobody talks about in aftersales

New battery packs leave the factory with a documented chain of evidence. Repaired packs often don't. A module swap or BMS re-flash can restore function — or introduce a latent fault that returns in 72 hours at an ESS site or bus depot. After seven years in battery and high-tech environments (Northvolt · Prodrive · Brainport), I believe repair quality needs production-grade discipline.

NMC and LFP fail differently

NMCLFP
Typical useEV, bus, mobilityESS, UPS, datacenter
Common returnsCell drift, thermal, HV wearBMS/CAN timeout, config, seals
QC focusIR, thermal verificationGolden firmware, connectors

My 8-step repair QC gate

  1. Incoming & ESD — quarantine if safety-critical
  2. HV isolation (IR) — before energization
  3. Cell / module balance — chemistry-specific ΔV
  4. BMS / CAN — separate comms from cell faults
  5. Thermal system — leak, pump, fan
  6. Repair to work instruction — torque, traceability
  7. Post-repair functional test
  8. Final release — SPC on pack voltage, full docs

Case study: BMS timeout on LFP ESS (not a board swap)

LFP ESS — Site reports CAN timeout. Root cause: connector moisture (pin 4–7 open) + wrong firmware profile after field re-flash. Fix: harness section + golden image re-flash + 4 h soak. Scale: mandatory connector photo on ESS intake; config backup before any BMS SW action. FC-02 down ~38% next quarter.

Five rules for high-volume lines

  • One defect → one permanent gate change (48 h)
  • Pre-kit bays for Pareto top 5
  • Golden images & torque logs on every HV job
  • Daily 15-min defect huddle (aftersales · production · dev)
  • FTTR ≥ 92%; repeat in 30 days → auto 8D
